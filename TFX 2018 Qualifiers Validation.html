TFX 2018 Qualifier Verification¶

Verification of the 2018 TFX Qualifier Competition Leaderboard

December 2017

Author¶

Dax Garner, dax.garner@gmail.com

Executive Summary¶

This analysis showcases that a large percentage of athlete's ranks are incorrect in the final results for the 2018 TFX Qualifer held in October 2017. Sadly, this has a significant potential to exclude qualifying athletes from the competition in January. The rankings for the 2018 TFX Qualfier competition are sloppy at best, and there is a high likelihood that qualifying athletes were not invited to the competition in January 2018 because their rankings are incorrect.

The most significant finding is that Amanda Garces may have been excluded from the Women's Pro Division due to incorrect rankings of other athletes in Events 1, 2 and 3. Because of the incorrect ranking system, Amanda Garces may have been excluded from winning thousands of dollars in January.

These findings shed light on inadequate data processing either by TFX and/or by the FloElite and needs to be resolved for the next competition.

Introduction¶

As a Crossfit athlete I recently competed in The 2018 Fittest Experience Qualifier back in October 2017. For more details on the competition, see the qualifier details. As a budding data scientist, I was interested in analyzing the data from the qualifier to help me understand my competition come January 2018. Thus, I set out with the question:

"What event(s) could I have made the smallest improvement to maximize my overall ranking?"

However, as I dug into the data, it quickly became a lesson is data cleaning with some interesting results. Here is what I found.

# Do something with those NaNs now that we have all the data
def clean_raw(table):
  for e in range(1, 7):
      col_rank = 'Q'+str(e)+'R'
      col_scor = 'Q'+str(e)+'S'
      table[col_rank].fillna(table[col_rank].max() + 1, inplace=True) # assumption
      table[col_scor].fillna(0.0, inplace=True) # assumption
  # Handle possible duplicate names
  table.index = table.index + table.groupby(level=0).cumcount().astype(str).replace('0','')
  table.index.rename('Athlete', inplace=True)
  return table
table = clean_raw(table)

Aside: Score-Rank Relationship¶

Before diving into the analysis, let's understand what the basic rules are in a Crossfit competition for score and resulting ranks.

Event Ranking¶

Each event has a scoring metric to assess how well each athlete completes the event. In the 2018 TFX Qualifier, events 1, 2, 4, 5, 6 were an As-Many-R[eps|ounds]-As-Possible (AMRAP) style workout. Event 3 was a maximum lift style workout. Thus, for all events, a high score (either in number of reps completed or weight lifted) is directly related to a high rank (low numerical value). For example in Event 1, Jean Banos completed a total of 344 repetitions, which is the highest score, and thus, was awarded a rank of 1.

Overall Ranking¶

Now, to assess the overall ranking of an athlete with respect to performance from all events, an athlete's Total Score is the sum of the the individual event ranks. Jonathan Vanover received 4th in event 1, 24th in event 2, 7th in event 3, etc. Thus, Jonathan Vanover's total score is 4+24+7+113+5+30 = 183 points. An athlete's overall Rank is then determined by their Total Score. A low Total Score results in a high overall Rank (low numerical value). Thus, Jonathan's 183 total points put him at first place in the Intermediate/Scaled Men's Division. Nice job Jonathan!

Additional Rules¶

The only other rule I am aware of is:

  • Athletes must complete and enter a score for all events to be ranked in the overall standings

This actually has an interesting implication in the analysis, but more on that later.

New Question¶

I set out with a question about sizing up my competition, but now I am deeply concerned with the validity of these results. The two outliers identified above were obvious, but are there other issues with the data that the plots are not able to showcase? Thus, I set out with a new question:

"Did all the qualifing athletes deserve to qualify?"

The whole point of the qualifier competition is to end up with a solid set of athletes to compete against in the actual TFX Competition in January 2018. If the rankings of these athletes is wrong, then were certain athletes missed and did not receive invitations? In some of the divisions, these athletes are competing for lots of money, the competition should do its due diligence to ensure the results are correct.

Re-ranking the Results¶

To double check the results, I decided to recompute the rankings by assuming the scores are all correctly entered. My hypothesis is that somewhere along the way, the sorting and ranking for some of the ranks got messed up. To assess the overall rankings, each event has to be re-ranked as well because the Total Score is based on the invididual event ranks. So, first, the individual events.

In [47]:
# Preserve Original
tfx_u = tfx.copy(deep=True)

# Re-Rank each Event
def rerank_events(tfx_u):
  for e in range(1, 7):
      col_rank = 'Q'+str(e)+'R'
      col_scor = 'Q'+str(e)+'S'
      tfx_u.sort_values(col_scor, ascending=False, inplace=True) # Max score is highest rank for each event
      tfx_u.reset_index(inplace=True)
      tfx_u.loc[:, col_rank] = tfx_u[col_scor].rank(ascending=False, method='min')
      tfx_u.set_index('Athlete', inplace=True)
  return tfx_u
tfx_u = rerank_events(tfx_u)
plot_events(tfx_u)

Next, re-ranking the overall results. Remember, there is a rule that for athletes to be ranked overall, they must complete all events. To apply the rule, anyone with a zero score for any of the events received the highest total score + 1. Thus, when the athletes are re-ranked based on their score, they will receive last place overall. It was a choice I made to simplify the analysis. I didn't wish to think through the logic on ranking athletes that didn't complete a subset of the events.

def verify_ranks(tfx, tfx_u, division):
  # Order the two frames athletes to be the exact same
  tfx_u = tfx_u.reindex(tfx.index)

  # Validate Ranks
  tfx_u.reset_index(inplace=True)
  tfx.reset_index(inplace=True)
  for e in range(1, 7):
      col_rank = 'Q'+str(e)+'R'
      col_new = 'Q'+str(e)+'RV'
      tfx[col_new] = np.where(tfx_u[col_rank] == tfx[col_rank], True, False)
  tfx['RankV'] = np.where(tfx_u['Rank'] == tfx['Rank'], True, False)
  tfx_u.set_index('Athlete', inplace=True)
  tfx.set_index('Athlete', inplace=True)
  #print(tfx.head())

  # Qualifying Athletes
  tfx.sort_values("Rank", inplace=True)
  tfx_u.sort_values("Rank", inplace=True)
  tfx.reset_index(inplace=True)
  tfx_u.reset_index(inplace=True)
  tfx_q = tfx.iloc[:dmap[division]["n_qualified"]].copy(deep=True)
  tfx_uq = tfx_u.iloc[:dmap[division]["n_qualified"]].copy(deep=True)
  tfx_uq.set_index('Athlete', inplace=True)
  tfx_q.set_index('Athlete', inplace=True)
  tfx_u.set_index('Athlete', inplace=True)
  tfx.set_index('Athlete', inplace=True)
  #print(tfx_q[['Total Score', 'Rank', 'RankV']])
  #print(tfx_uq[['Total Score', 'Rank']])
  tfx_uq.sort_index(inplace=True)
  tfx_q.sort_index(inplace=True)
  #print(tfx_q.index)
  #print(tfx_uq.index)
  deserving = np.all(tfx_q.index == tfx_uq.index)
  print("Did all qualifying athletes deserve to qualify? {}\n"\
          .format(deserving))
  ath_q_des = [i for i in tfx_uq.index if i not in tfx_q.index]
  ath_q_undes = [i for i in tfx_q.index if i not in tfx_uq.index]
  if not deserving:
      print("Athletes ranked outside the qualifier cut-off, but actually qualified:")
      print(ath_q_des)
      print('')
      print("Athletes ranked within the qualifier cut-off, but didn't actually qualified:")
      print(ath_q_undes)
      print('')
  return tfx, tfx_u, tfx_q, tfx_uq

  tfx_ve = []

      col_v = 'Q'+str(e)+'RV'

  tfx_wq = tfx_wq[tfx_wq['RankV'] == False]

def verify(division, plot_results=True, apply_rules=True):
  table = clean_raw(extract(division))
  tfx = table.copy(deep=True)

Conclusion¶

I have demonstrated through this analysis that a large percentage of athlete's ranks are incorrect in the final results for the 2018 TFX Qualifer, which has a significant potential to exclude qualifying athletes from the competition in January. The most significant finding is that Amanda Garces may have been excluded from the Women's Pro Division due to incorrect rankings of other athletes in Events 1, 2 and 3. Because of the incorrect ranking system, Amanda Garces may have been excluded from winning thousands of dollars in January!

The rankings for the 2018 TFX Qualfier competition are sloppy at best, and there is a high likelihood that athletes could have been invited to the TFX competition in January but were not because their rankings are incorrect. TFX nor the FloElite leaderboard indicate who has accepted invitations to TFX in January and athletes can decline their invitiations.

The athletes that compete in TFX put their heart, soul and sweat into the competition. So, I call upon the organizers of The Fittest Experience to put in the due diligence to ensure the results are correct as well from now on.
